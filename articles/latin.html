History of the Latin Language

Latin began as the language of the Latins ‚Äî a people living in the region of Latium in central Italy. As Rome grew from a small city-state to a vast empire, Latin spread across the entire Mediterranean world, displacing local languages.

Latin Grammar: An Overview

Latin is a highly inflected language ‚Äî meaning that word endings change to show grammatical function (subject, object, possessive, etc.) rather than relying on word order as English does. This system of endings is called declension for nouns and conjugation for verbs.

Latin and Christianity

Latin became the dominant language of the Western Christian Church. The Vulgate ‚Äî St. Jerome's Latin translation of the Bible (completed c. AD 405) ‚Äî was the standard Christian scripture in the West for over a thousand years. The Catholic Mass was celebrated in Latin until the Second Vatican Council (1962‚Äì1965).

Many important theological and philosophical terms used in Christian discourse ‚Äî including by Latter-day Saint teachers and scholars ‚Äî come directly from Latin: atonement (expiatio), salvation (salus), grace (gratia), resurrection (resurrectio), revelation (revelatio).

Latin in English

Approximately 60% of English vocabulary derives from Latin, either directly or through French (which is itself a Romance language descended from Latin). Latin roots, prefixes, and suffixes are essential tools for vocabulary building.

Why Study Latin?

Latin study sharpens the mind, improves English vocabulary, aids in learning all the Romance languages, and opens access to some of the greatest literature and theology ever written. C.S. Lewis and J.R.R. Tolkien ‚Äî both deeply influential Christian thinkers ‚Äî were formed by classical Latin education. Joseph Smith himself referenced scripture in Latin contexts, and many early Latter-day Saint scholars valued classical languages.

Classical education ‚Äî including Latin ‚Äî has experienced a strong revival in homeschool and Christian school settings, partly because it trains careful, logical, systematic thinking that benefits all areas of study.
