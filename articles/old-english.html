Origins of Old English

Old English developed from the Germanic dialects brought to Britain by the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes ‚Äî peoples from what is now northern Germany and Denmark ‚Äî who began settling Britain in the 5th century AD after the Roman legions withdrew. Over time, their dialects merged into a common language that would become English.

The language was strongly shaped by Christianity. When Augustine of Canterbury brought Christianity to Anglo-Saxon England in AD 597, Latin learning flourished. Monks translated scriptures and wrote history in Old English, creating a rich literary tradition.

Beowulf

Beowulf is the greatest surviving work of Old English literature ‚Äî a 3,182-line epic poem describing the hero Beowulf's battles against the monster Grendel, Grendel's mother, and a dragon. It is among the oldest poems in any Germanic language.

The poem has a distinctly Christian overlay on older pagan material ‚Äî the monsters are described as descendants of the biblical Cain, and God's providence is acknowledged throughout. This blending of Christian faith and heroic tradition is characteristic of Anglo-Saxon culture.

J.R.R. Tolkien's landmark 1936 essay, "Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics," rescued Beowulf from being treated merely as a historical document and established it as a great work of literature deserving to be read on its own terms. Tolkien later produced his own translation of the poem.

Old English and Christianity

The Anglo-Saxon church produced remarkable Christian literature. Bede (c. 673‚Äì735), the "Father of English History," wrote the Ecclesiastical History of the English People. King Alfred the Great (849‚Äì899) translated portions of the Bible and other Christian works into Old English to make them accessible to his people, in a manner reminiscent of Joseph Smith's mission to make the word of God plain and accessible to all.

The Lindisfarne Gospels (c. AD 715‚Äì720) ‚Äî a beautifully illuminated Latin Gospel book ‚Äî were later given an Old English interlinear translation, one of the earliest vernacular Gospel translations in history.

Old English's Legacy

After the Norman Conquest of 1066, Old English was gradually replaced by Middle English ‚Äî a hybrid of Old English and the Norman French of the conquerors. Yet the core vocabulary of English (articles, pronouns, basic verbs, numbers, body parts) remains overwhelmingly Old English in origin. Every English speaker, whether they know it or not, speaks a language built on an Anglo-Saxon foundation.
