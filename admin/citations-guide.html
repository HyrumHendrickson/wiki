Citations Guide

Last updated: February 27, 2026 EduWiki Admin Admin Citations References
Admin Article — This guide explains how to add citations and references to EduWiki articles.
Summary Every EduWiki article should cite its sources. Citations are added in a numbered list at the bottom of the article, and inline references in the body text (like [1]) automatically become clickable superscript links that jump to the citation.

Why Citations Matter

EduWiki is designed for teaching, and academic integrity is important. Citations:

  • Give credit to the original authors of ideas and content.
  • Allow readers to verify facts and explore further.
  • Build trust in the accuracy of the article.
  • Provide deeper reading for students who want to learn more.

The Citations Section

Every article must end with a wiki-citations section, just before the closing </main> tag: 

<section class="wiki-citations" aria-labelledby="citations-heading">
  <h2 id="citations-heading">References</h2>
  <ol>
    <li>Author, A. (Year). <em>Title of Book</em>. Publisher.</li>
    <li>Author, B. (Year). "Article Title." <em>Journal Name</em>, Vol(Issue), pages.</li>
    <li>Website Name. <a href="https://..." target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">url</a></li>
  </ol>
</section>

Inline Citation References

To link a claim in the body text to a specific citation, write [1], [2], etc. EduWiki's JavaScript automatically converts these to clickable superscript links: 

<p>
  The Pythagorean theorem was known to Babylonian mathematicians as early as 1800 BC. [1]
  The Greek philosopher Pythagoras is credited with the first formal proof. [2]
</p>

The numbers link to the corresponding entry in the references list at the bottom of the page. The numbers must match the order of items in your <ol>.

Citation Formats

EduWiki loosely follows APA style. Here are templates for common source types:

Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year).
<em>Title of book: Subtitle if any</em> (Edition ed.).
Publisher.

Example:

Stewart, J. (2015). <em>Calculus: Early Transcendentals</em> (8th ed.). Cengage Learning.
Author, A. A. (Year). "Article title."
<em>Journal Name</em>, Volume(Issue), page–page.
https://doi.org/xxxxx (if available)

Example:

Robson, E. (2002). "Words and Pictures: New Light on Plimpton 322."
<em>American Mathematical Monthly</em>, 109(2), 105–120.
Author or Organization. (Year, Month Day). <em>Page title</em>.
Website Name. <a href="URL" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">URL</a>

Example:

Khan Academy. (2024). <em>Calculus</em>.
<a href="https://www.khanacademy.org/math/calculus-1" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">
  khanacademy.org/math/calculus-1
</a>
Creator. (Year, Month Day). <em>Video title</em> [Video].
Platform. <a href="URL" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">URL</a>

Example:

3Blue1Brown. (2017, April 28). <em>The Essence of Calculus</em> [Video].
YouTube. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WUvTyaaNkzM" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">
  youtube.com/watch?v=WUvTyaaNkzM
</a>

Minimal Citation Requirements

Every article must include:

  • At least one citation in the references section.
  • An inline reference [1] wherever a major factual claim is made.
  • A link (with target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer") for all online sources.

For admin/help articles, a single citation like "EduWiki internal documentation." is acceptable.

Complete Example

<!-- In the body text: -->
<p>
  Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz independently developed calculus
  in the 17th century. [1] The notation we use today is primarily
  due to Leibniz. [2]
</p>

<!-- At the bottom of the article: -->
<section class="wiki-citations" aria-labelledby="citations-heading">
  <h2 id="citations-heading">References</h2>
  <ol>
    <li>
      Boyer, C. B. (1959). <em>The History of the Calculus and Its
      Conceptual Development</em>. Dover.
    </li>
    <li>
      Cajori, F. (1929). <em>A History of Mathematical Notations</em>.
      Open Court Publishing.
    </li>
  </ol>
</section>
