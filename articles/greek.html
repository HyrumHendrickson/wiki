Periods of Ancient Greek

The Archaic period gave us Homer's Iliad and Odyssey (c. 800 BC), the foundational works of Western literature. The Classical period produced the golden age of Athens: Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle in philosophy; Sophocles and Euripides in drama; Thucydides in history. Classical Attic Greek is considered the most elegant and complex form of the language.

Following Alexander the Great's conquests, a simplified, common form of Greek â€” Koine (ÎºÎ¿Î¹Î½Î®, "common") â€” spread across the Mediterranean world and became the language of trade, government, and culture. God chose this moment in history to send His Son, ensuring that the gospel could spread rapidly in a common language.

The entire New Testament was written in Koine Greek. Learning even basic Greek allows students to read Matthew, Mark, John, and Paul in their original words â€” often clarifying meanings that translations can only approximate.

Greek Philosophy and Christian Thought

Greek philosophy â€” especially Plato and Aristotle â€” profoundly influenced Christian theology through thinkers like Justin Martyr, Clement of Alexandria, and Augustine. However, Latter-day Saint theology takes a distinctive approach: while it engages philosophical reasoning, it insists that revelation rather than philosophy is the ultimate source of truth about God. [1]

The incorporation of Greek philosophical categories (like "substance," "essence," and "immutability") into early Christian creeds is what Latter-day Saints identify as part of the Apostasy â€” a movement away from the simple, personal God of scripture toward an abstract philosophical construct.

Why Study Greek Today?

For any serious student of the New Testament, Greek is an invaluable tool. Even a basic knowledge of Koine Greek allows you to:

  • Read the words of Jesus and the Apostles without a translation layer
  • Understand why different Bible translations differ
  • Catch nuances of meaning in passages that are ambiguous in English
  • Engage more deeply with Christian apologetics and theology
