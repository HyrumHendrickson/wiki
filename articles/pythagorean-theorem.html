The Pythagorean Theorem

üìÖ Last updated: February 27, 2026 ‚úç EduWiki Team Mathematics Geometry
Summary The Pythagorean Theorem is one of the most fundamental results in Euclidean geometry. It states that in any right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides. In formula form: $$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$ where $c$ is the length of the hypotenuse and $a$, $b$ are the legs of the triangle.

Statement of the Theorem

For any right triangle with legs of length $a$ and $b$ and hypotenuse of length $c$:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

The hypotenuse is always the side opposite the right angle and is the longest side of the triangle.

Proofs

The Pythagorean Theorem has more known proofs than any other theorem in mathematics ‚Äî over 370 distinct proofs have been documented. Below are three of the most instructive.

Arrange four identical right triangles (legs $a$, $b$; hypotenuse $c$) inside a large square of side $(a + b)$.

The large square has area $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$.
The four triangles have total area $4 \cdot \frac{1}{2}ab = 2ab$.
The inner shape (also a square, with side $c$) has area $c^2$.

Therefore:

$$(a+b)^2 - 2ab = c^2 \implies a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \quad \checkmark$$

Drop an altitude $h$ from the right angle to the hypotenuse, dividing it into segments $p$ and $q$ where $p + q = c$.

The altitude creates two smaller triangles, each similar to the original. From the similarity ratios:

$$a^2 = c \cdot p \quad \text{and} \quad b^2 = c \cdot q$$

Adding:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c(p + q) = c \cdot c = c^2 \quad \checkmark$$

Let $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ be two perpendicular vectors. Then $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$.

$$|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) \cdot (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + |\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

This generalizes the Pythagorean Theorem to any number of dimensions.

Pythagorean Triples

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three positive integers $(a, b, c)$ satisfying $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. The simplest example is $(3, 4, 5)$:

$$3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5^2$$

Generating Formula

All primitive Pythagorean triples can be generated by choosing integers $m > n > 0$ with $\gcd(m,n)=1$ and $m - n$ odd:

$$a = m^2 - n^2, \quad b = 2mn, \quad c = m^2 + n^2$$

The Converse

The converse of the Pythagorean Theorem is also true: if $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, then the triangle with sides $a$, $b$, $c$ is a right triangle. This gives a useful test:

  • If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$: right triangle
  • If $a^2 + b^2 > c^2$: acute triangle
  • If $a^2 + b^2 < c^2$: obtuse triangle

Extensions and Generalizations

For a triangle with sides $a$, $b$, $c$ and the angle $C$ opposite side $c$:

$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$$

When $C = 90¬∞$, $\cos C = 0$, and this reduces to $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ ‚Äî the Pythagorean Theorem.

The distance between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ in 2D is:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$$

In 3D, between $(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2,z_2)$:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2 + (z_2-z_1)^2}$$

Both are direct applications of the Pythagorean Theorem.

Historical Background

Although the theorem bears the name of the Greek philosopher Pythagoras (c. 570‚Äì495 BC), evidence of the relationship was known to Babylonian and Indian mathematicians much earlier. The Babylonian tablet Plimpton 322 (c. 1800 BC) lists Pythagorean triples, suggesting knowledge of the theorem over a millennium before Pythagoras. [1][2]

The first rigorous proof, however, is attributed to the Pythagorean school of ancient Greece. [3]
