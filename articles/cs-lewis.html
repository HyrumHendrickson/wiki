C.S. Lewis

Last updated: February 27, 2026 EduWiki Team Literature Apologetics Christianity
Summary Clive Staples Lewis (1898–1963) was a British author, Oxford don, and one of the most influential Christian writers of the 20th century. A former atheist who was converted to Christianity in part through conversations with J.R.R. Tolkien, Lewis became a brilliant defender of rational Christian faith. His works — from Mere Christianity to The Chronicles of Narnia — have profoundly influenced millions of readers, including many Latter-day Saints.

From Atheism to Christianity

Lewis grew up in a nominally religious household but became an atheist as a teenager. He served in World War I, then studied at Oxford, where he became a Fellow and tutor. Through years of intellectual struggle — reading Christian philosophers like George MacDonald, discussing faith with J.R.R. Tolkien, and following the logic of his own arguments — Lewis was converted to theism in 1929 and to Christianity in 1931.

He later described his conversion as being "the most dejected and reluctant convert in all England" — God closing in on him from every intellectual direction until he could no longer honestly refuse to believe. His intellectual journey is described in his autobiography, Surprised by Joy (1955).

Major Works

Originally a series of BBC radio broadcasts during World War II, Mere Christianity is Lewis's systematic defense of basic Christian faith, aimed at thoughtful non-believers. Key arguments include the Moral Law argument (the existence of a universal moral sense points to a moral Lawgiver) and the famous "Liar, Lunatic, or Lord" trilemma: Jesus was either a great deceiver, a madman, or exactly who He claimed to be.

Many Latter-day Saints have found Mere Christianity helpful as an introduction to rational Christian apologetics, while recognizing that it stops short of the restored gospel's fullness.

A satirical masterpiece told from the perspective of a senior devil (Screwtape) writing letters of advice to his junior devil nephew (Wormwood) on how to tempt a human "patient." The book brilliantly exposes how temptation works — through distraction, gradual compromise, and the subtle corruption of good things — making it as practically useful today as when it was written.

Seven novels set in the magical land of Narnia, where the great lion Aslan — a Christ figure — rules and redeems. Lewis used fantasy to communicate theological truths in a way that could reach children and adults who might be resistant to direct religious argument. The death and resurrection of Aslan in The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe is one of the most moving depictions of the Atonement in modern fiction.

Lewis and the "Longing" Argument (Sehnsucht)

One of Lewis's most distinctive and powerful arguments for the existence of God is the argument from desire, or Sehnsucht (the German word for a deep longing or yearning). Lewis observed that human beings have a persistent longing for something no earthly thing can satisfy — a joy that is "the inconsolable longing" for we know not what.

His argument: "If I find in myself desires which nothing in this world can satisfy, the only logical explanation is that I was made for another world." This resonates powerfully with Latter-day Saint doctrine that we are eternal beings who lived before this life with our Heavenly Father, and that our homesickness for the divine is literally a memory.

C.S. Lewis and Latter-day Saint Thought

C.S. Lewis is widely read and admired among Latter-day Saints. Several of his theological intuitions — while coming from a different tradition — strikingly parallel LDS doctrine:

  • Theosis / Becoming like God: In Mere Christianity, Lewis writes: "The command 'Be ye perfect' is not idealistic gas. Nor is it a command to do the impossible. He is going to make us into creatures that can obey that command." This echoes LDS doctrine of eternal progression.
  • A personal, accessible God: Lewis rejected abstract philosophical deism and insisted on a God who is personal, responsive, and genuinely loves each person.
  • The reality of the afterlife: Lewis took heaven and hell literally and seriously — not as metaphors but as real destinations.

Latter-day Saint scholars and leaders have frequently quoted Lewis. Elder Neal A. Maxwell and others found his apologetic approach deeply compatible with restored gospel principles.
