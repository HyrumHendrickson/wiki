Introduction to Calculus

Summary Calculus is the branch of mathematics studying continuous change. It was developed independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the 17th century. Calculus has two major sub-disciplines: differential calculus, which concerns rates of change and slopes of curves, and integral calculus, which concerns accumulation of quantities and areas under curves. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus links these two branches.

Limits

The concept of a limit is the foundation of calculus. A limit describes the value that a function approaches as the input approaches a given point. We write:

$$\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$$

This reads: "the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $c$ equals $L$." The function does not need to be defined at $c$ ‚Äî the limit only describes the approach.

Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$.

Direct substitution gives $\frac{0}{0}$, which is indeterminate. We factor:

$$\frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2$$

Now we can substitute: $\lim_{x \to 2}(x + 2) = 4$.

Derivatives

The derivative of a function $f$ at a point $x$ measures the instantaneous rate of change ‚Äî essentially, the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point. The formal definition is:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

Find the derivative of $f(x) = 4x^3 - 2x^2 + 7x - 1$.

Apply the power rule to each term:

$$f'(x) = 12x^2 - 4x + 7$$

Note that the derivative of the constant $-1$ is $0$, and the derivative of $7x$ is $7$.

Integrals

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. The definite integral of $f(x)$ from $a$ to $b$ computes the signed area between the curve and the $x$-axis:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx$$

The indefinite integral (antiderivative) reverses differentiation:

$$\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) connects differentiation and integration. It has two parts:

If $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt$, then

$$F'(x) = f(x)$$

In words: differentiation and integration are inverse operations.

If $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ on $[a, b]$, then

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

Example: $\int_0^2 x^2\, dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^2 = \frac{8}{3} - 0 = \frac{8}{3}$

Applications

Calculus has applications across virtually every field of science and engineering:

  • Physics: velocity and acceleration are derivatives of position.
  • Engineering: optimization of designs using critical points.
  • Economics: marginal cost and revenue are derivatives of cost functions.
  • Biology: population growth models use differential equations.
  • Computer Science: machine learning relies on gradient descent, a calculus technique.

A ball is thrown upward with initial velocity $v_0 = 20$ m/s. Its height is modeled by:

$$h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 20t$$

Find the maximum height by setting $h'(t) = 0$:

$$h'(t) = -9.8t + 20 = 0 \implies t = \frac{20}{9.8} \approx 2.04 \text{ s}$$

Maximum height: $h(2.04) \approx -4.9(2.04)^2 + 20(2.04) \approx 20.4$ m
